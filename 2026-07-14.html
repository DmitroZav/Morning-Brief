☀️ Big Story of the Day
While everyone waits for a rate cut that isn't coming, Canada's bank regulator quietly unlocked $74 billion for the Big Six to lend

Everybody's eyes are on the Bank of Canada tomorrow. But the move that actually loosens things up already happened last week — and almost nobody outside the industry noticed. Canada has a second, less famous regulator called OSFI (the Office of the Superintendent of Financial Institutions — think of it as the referee that makes sure the big banks don't take crazy risks). One of its main dials is a rainy-day cushion it forces the six biggest banks to hold, called the Domestic Stability Buffer. OSFI just cut that cushion for the first time in three years, dropping it from 3.5% to 3.0%.

In plain English: that one change frees up roughly $74 billion of capital the Big Six (RBC, TD, BMO, Scotiabank, CIBC, National Bank) can now put to work instead of sitting on. The regulator is basically telling the banks, "go ahead, take a bit more risk, lend more." That doesn't lower your rate — but it can quietly make banks hungrier for your mortgage: more willing to approve, more willing to compete for your business. It's the opposite signal to the doom you keep hearing.

Video-worthy because: your whole audience is glued to the wrong screen. Tomorrow's rate hold is a non-event; the real easing came through a side door most people have never heard of. Being the guy who explains that in plain language is pure trust-builder content.
