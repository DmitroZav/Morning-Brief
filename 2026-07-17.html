☀️ Big Story of the Day
The war on your news feed is quietly showing up in your mortgage

Oil is spiking again. Fresh attacks on ships in the Strait of Hormuz — the narrow sea lane that carries about one in five barrels of the world's oil — pushed West Texas crude above US$79 a barrel this week, up more than 11%. Here's the chain that reaches your wallet: pricier oil means higher inflation fears, higher inflation fears push up government bond yields (the interest the government pays to borrow money), and bond yields are what lenders use to price fixed mortgage rates. So the fighting overseas is landing, a few steps later, on the mortgage you're about to renew or take out.

The damage is already visible. Since this conflict flared, the best 5-year fixed rates have crept up roughly 0.35 to 0.40 of a percentage point and now mostly sit above 4%. The Bank of Canada didn't do this — it held its rate two days ago and hasn't moved. This is the oil market doing it, over the Bank's head.

Video-worthy because: everyone's doom-scrolling the war as a foreign-news story. Almost nobody has connected it to their own mortgage payment. 🧠 Why It Matters (To You)

Buying or renewing this summer: This is the one to act on. Fixed rates are drifting up because of oil, not the Bank. If a lender has offered you a fixed rate you can live with, ask how long they'll hold it — many honour a quote for 90–120 days. Locking a hold protects you if the war keeps pushing rates up, and you can usually still take a lower rate if things calm down. Waiting and hoping is the risky move this month.

First-time buyer: GTA condos are the clearest value in the market — resale prices down about 9.4% from a year ago, roughly $65k off a typical condo. And with brand-new condo construction frozen at a 35-year low, the supply coming in future years is shrinking. A soft price today plus a thinner pipeline tomorrow is a real setup to think about — if your own numbers work.

Renter: A frozen construction pipeline is the part that touches you. Fewer new units built now means less rental supply in two or three years, which usually props rents back up over time. Rents have drifted down lately — if you find a place you like at today's softer rent, a longer lease can lock in the discount before supply tightens again.

Newcomer: Tune out the war panic as a real-estate signal, but understand the one real link: it's nudging fixed mortgage rates up, not down. Don't plan your purchase around a rate cut that keeps not arriving. Plan around today's ~4% reality, get a real pre-approval so a lender holds a rate for you, and buy when your numbers work — not when a headline tells you to.
