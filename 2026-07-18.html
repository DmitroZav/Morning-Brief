☀️ Big Story of the Day
Trump just raised tariffs on Canada to 35% — and the loonie fell off a cliff

Yesterday President Trump signed an executive order lifting the tariff on Canadian goods to 35%, up from 25%. A tariff is a tax the importing country charges on goods crossing the border — so American buyers now pay 35% more for a lot of what Canada sells them. Goods that qualify under CUSMA (the Canada–US–Mexico free trade deal) are still exempt, which softens the blow. But lumber and auto parts got hit hard, and business groups in Ontario and Quebec started warning about layoffs within hours.

The market reaction was instant. The Canadian dollar dropped to about 72 US cents — a two-year low. Prime Minister Carney said he was "disappointed," left the door open to a deal, and retaliated in an unusual way: he's withholding Canada's share of the toll revenue from the Windsor–Detroit bridge until Canada recovers its investment.

Why this reaches your kitchen table: a weaker loonie makes everything Canada imports more expensive — groceries, fuel, cars, electronics. That's inflation. And more inflation means the Bank of Canada has less room to cut interest rates, which is the thing most of us are quietly waiting for. Meanwhile a manufacturing layoff wave in Ontario is exactly the kind of job-security shock that keeps buyers frozen.

Video-worthy because: everyone saw the "35% tariff" headline. Almost nobody has connected it to their own mortgage renewal or grocery bill. Be the person who draws the line from a Washington signature to their monthly payment.

🎯 The One Thing
The 35% tariff and a 72-cent loonie just made a Bank of Canada rate cut less likely, not more. If you've been waiting for cheaper money before you buy — that wait got longer yesterday.

🧠 Why It Matters (To You)

If you're a first-time buyer: prices in the GTA are still down about 4% from a year ago and sellers have less competition than they think — new listings fell 12.9%. But the "wait for the Bank of Canada to cut" strategy just got weaker. Tariff-driven inflation is the exact thing that keeps rates where they are. Buying power today is a known quantity; buying power in six months is a guess.

If you're renting: here's the quiet story. Almost 6 in 10 new homes being built in Canada right now are purpose-built rentals, not condos to own. More rental supply is genuinely good news for your rent over the next few years. It's also a signal about who developers think can actually afford to buy.

If you're a newcomer: a 72-cent loonie means money you send home goes further, and money you bring here buys less. And if you work in manufacturing, auto parts, or lumber in Ontario — watch your industry closely this month. Job security is the number one thing lenders look at.
