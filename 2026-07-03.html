☀️ Big Story of the Day
Toronto's housing market just flashed a turn — sales jumped 9.4% and the board says prices are next

This morning TRREB (the Toronto Regional Real Estate Board — the group that tracks every home sale in the GTA) dropped its June numbers, and they're the strongest signal we've seen all year. GTA agents sold 6,770 homes in June, up 9.4% from a year ago. New listings actually fell 12.9%. More buyers, fewer homes coming to market — that's the exact combination that pushes prices up.

Prices haven't caught up yet: the average June price was $1,058,658, still down 3.9% from last year. But here's the part that matters — on a month-to-month basis (stripping out seasonal noise), prices actually ticked up, and the board is now openly predicting "renewed price growth" in the back half of 2026 as pent-up demand comes off the sidelines.

Video-worthy because: This is the freshest, most local, most wallet-relevant story of the day for your audience. For two years everyone's been waiting for "the bottom." Today the biggest board in the country is basically saying the window is starting to close. That's a stop-scrolling headline.

🧠 Why It Matters (To You)

If you're a first-time buyer: the "wait forever for a crash" plan just got riskier. Sales are up, listings are down, and the biggest board in the country is calling for prices to rise. You don't have to panic-buy — but the deep-discount window that's been open for two years is showing its first real signs of closing. If you're mortgage-approved and ready, this is the summer to be serious, not passive.

If you're a renter: rents are still falling (20 months straight), so renting isn't a trap right now — it can be a smart way to stack a bigger down payment while the sale market heats up. Just know the math is shifting: every month prices firm up is a month a bigger deposit buys you less house.

If you're a newcomer: this is your reminder that "Toronto is only ever going down" was never true — it moves in cycles, and we may be watching one turn. Get your financing sorted and your paperwork ready now, so you can move when you find the right place instead of scrambling.

Full Script (target ~45s)

Hook: When I moved to Canada, nobody explained condo maintenance fees to me. It's the number that quietly kills first-time buyer budgets — here's how it works.

Body: Every condo has a monthly fee on top of your mortgage. It pays for the building — heating, water, the gym, the concierge, snow removal, and a "reserve fund," which is basically the building's savings account for big repairs like the roof or elevators. In 2026, these fees are climbing fast because construction and repair costs went up, and a lot of older buildings underfunded that reserve for years. So here's the one question to ask before you buy: is the reserve fund healthy? Ask for the reserve fund study. If the building's savings account is thin, you can get hit with a "special assessment" — a surprise bill of thousands of dollars — even after you move in. A cheaper condo with a broke reserve fund is not a deal. It's a trap.
