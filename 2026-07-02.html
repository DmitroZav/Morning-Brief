☀️ Big Story of the Day
Canada's trade deal with the US just hit its deadline — no deal, tariffs stay

Yesterday, July 1, was the review deadline for CUSMA — the free-trade agreement that shields nearly 90% of Canadian exports from Trump's tariffs. (CUSMA = the Canada-US-Mexico Agreement, the modern version of NAFTA.) The US declined to lock in a fresh long-term deal. Instead of a clean renewal, the whole thing moves into a slow 10-year review that could leave it in limbo. In plain terms: the tariffs that have been hurting our auto, steel and aluminum industries aren't going away.

Why should a normal person care? Because economists now peg the damage at more than $60 billion over the next year, with Canada's growth forecast for 2026 sitting at a weak 0.7%. A slow economy touches jobs, the Canadian dollar, and — the part that matters for housing — what the Bank of Canada does with interest rates next.

Video-worthy because: It's the biggest Canada story in the last 24 hours, it's confusing (which means people want it explained simply), and it connects straight to two things your audience feels — their job and their mortgage.

🧠 Why It Matters (To You)

If you have a variable mortgage: a slower economy is actually your friend in the short term. When growth is weak, the Bank of Canada leans toward cutting rates, not raising them — so the CUSMA mess makes a rate cut later this year more likely, not less. That's a strange silver lining.

If you're a buyer: watch your own job security more than the headlines. The tariffs hit specific sectors hardest — auto, auto parts, steel, aluminum. If you work in or near one of those, factor that into how big a mortgage you take on right now.

If you're a newcomer: a weaker dollar makes money you bring in from abroad go a little further here, but it also makes imported goods pricier. And the bigger point — Canada's economy is stuck in low gear (0.7% growth), so don't count on a booming job market to bail out a stretched budget. Buy what you can actually carry.

Body: Here's the rule almost nobody explains to newcomers. In Canada, when you sell the home you actually live in — your principal residence — you pay zero tax on the profit. If you bought for 600 thousand and sold for 900, that 300 thousand gain is yours, tax-free. That's one of the biggest wealth builders in the country. Now, every so often you'll hear economists and some policy people call that gain "untaxed wealth" and float the idea of taxing it to make things fairer. So should you panic? No. There is no such tax today, and touching the principal-residence exemption would be political poison — no government wants to be the one that taxed people's homes. But it's smart to know the rule exists, because it's a real reason owning beats renting long-term in this country. Knowledge is your protection.
