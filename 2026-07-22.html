☀️ Big Story of the Day
Inflation just cooled to 2.8% — the fastest slowdown since the Iran ceasefire

Statistics Canada reported Monday that consumer prices rose 2.8% in June, down from 3.2% in May. The big driver was gas: still up year over year, but "only" 20.5% versus a punishing 33.2% the month before, after the Middle East ceasefire briefly let fuel flow again. Food inflation eased to 3.5%, and shelter costs slowed to 1.5%.

The quieter, bigger news is underneath the headline. The Bank of Canada's two favourite "core" inflation measures — the ones that strip out jumpy stuff like gas — fell to their lowest in more than five years (1.9% and 1.8%). Plain version: the underlying heat in the economy is actually coming out.

Why it's video-worthy: Everyone feels grocery and gas prices — this is a story a person who never reads business news still cares about. And there's a twist nobody's explaining: inflation cooled, but fixed mortgage rates did NOT fall. That gap is your whole video (see The One Thing).

🎯 The One Thing
Inflation dropped to 2.8% — but your fixed mortgage rate didn't move. Here's the disconnect most people get wrong.

People assume "inflation down = my rate goes down." Not this time. Fixed mortgage rates are priced off the 5-year Government of Canada bond yield — the interest the government pays to borrow for 5 years — and that yield is still stuck near 3.1%, held up by global tension and worries about U.S. inflation, not by our own numbers. So even with a great inflation report, the best 5-year fixed is around 3.94% and most lenders are still above 4%. The Bank of Canada controls the overnight rate (variable); the bond market controls your fixed. They don't always move together.

🧠 Why It Matters (To You)

If you're renewing or buying: Don't wait for the "inflation is down" headline to hand you a cheaper fixed rate — it already came and your rate didn't move. Fixed is tied to the bond market, and the bond market is nervous about the world, not about our grocery bills. If you need certainty, the ~3.94% is real today; if you can stomach risk, variable is cheaper but rides with the Bank of Canada.

If you're renting: Asking rents are actually falling in Toronto, Vancouver, Calgary and Ottawa right now — good news if you're signing a new lease. But housing starts fell again (−13% from last June). Fewer homes started today means fewer finished in 2028–2029, which is when this softer-rent window can slam shut.

If you're new to Canada: Two things landed this week for you — a cooler cost of living, and Ontario quietly rewriting its immigration program (see Content Play). If your plan was to work here and get nominated for permanent residence through OINP, the rules just changed. That affects where you can afford to plant roots.
