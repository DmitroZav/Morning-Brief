☀️ Big Story of the Day
The two things that were scaring the Bank of Canada just quietly disappeared — and it kills the "wait for a cheaper mortgage" plan

Back in the spring the Bank of Canada was stuck in a genuine bind. Two opposite dangers were pulling at it. On one side, inflation was jumping — the war in the Middle East had pushed oil above US$100 a barrel, gas prices spiked, and headline inflation climbed to 3.2% in May. On the other side, the economy looked weak and needed help. A central bank cuts rates to rescue a weak economy and raises them to cool off inflation — so it was pinned in the middle and did nothing.

Over the last month, both of those fears melted away. Oil has fallen from over US$100 all the way back into the US$70 range, so the inflation scare is fading (core inflation — the underlying trend once you strip out wild swings like gasoline — is back down near 2%). At the same time the economy quietly got stronger, not weaker: GDP bounced back 0.5% in April, Canada's trade surplus hit a four-year high in May, and unemployment actually ticked down to 6.5% in June. So the Bank no longer has a reason to cut (economy's fine) or a reason to hike (inflation's cooling). Translation: this Wednesday, July 15, it almost certainly holds at 2.25% for the sixth straight time.

Video-worthy because: half your audience is still sitting on the sidelines waiting for the Bank to cut so their mortgage gets cheaper. This is the plain-language moment to tell them the reasons for a cut just evaporated — the waiting game is officially over, and the real movement is somewhere else.
