CPI DAY — 8:30 A.M. ET
☀️ Big Story of the Day
Inflation day: the number that sets your grocery bill and your mortgage lands this morning

At 8:30 a.m. Eastern, Statistics Canada releases the June Consumer Price Index — CPI, the official measure of how fast prices are rising. May came in at 3.2%, the fastest since December 2023, driven almost entirely by gasoline (up 33.2% from a year earlier after the Strait of Hormuz closure). Economists polled by Reuters expect June to cool to about 2.9% as gas prices came back down.

This is the rare economic release that touches everybody. It is why your groceries cost what they cost — food at stores was up 4.3% in May, with fresh vegetables up 9.0%. And it is the input that moves bond yields, which set fixed mortgage rates.

Why it's video-worthy: Time-bound. You can post before 8:30 telling people what to watch for, then post the actual number after. Two videos, one story, and a reason for people to follow you today rather than tomorrow.

🎯 The One Thing
June inflation drops at 8:30 a.m. — expected to fall to 2.9% from 3.2%. If it lands soft, the pressure on fixed mortgage rates comes off. If it lands hot, that 3.99% five-year fixed quietly disappears.

Remember the chain: oil up → inflation up → bond yields up → fixed mortgage rates up. The Bank of Canada already told us it's on hold. The bond market is the thing actually setting your fixed rate right now.

🧠 Why It Matters (To You)

If you're buying: Today at 8:30 you get a real signal about where fixed rates go this fall. Sales in the GTA are up 9.4% year over year while new listings are down 12.9% — more competition, less choice. Prices are still down ~4% from last year. The window where you get both a soft price and a sub-4% rate is not permanent.

If you're renting: Here's the number that should worry you more than any price chart: 58.2% of new construction is now purpose-built rental, and building for ownership has collapsed to levels last seen in 2009. On top of that, seven Ontario transit-station housing projects — 9,165 homes — just got delayed by anywhere from a few months to three-plus years. Fewer homes finished in 2029 means your rent in 2029.

If you're new to Canada: Two things landed for you this month. Ontario tore up all nine of its old immigration streams on May 30 and replaced them with one Ontario Workforce Priority stream — so if you were building a plan around the old program, that plan is gone and you need a new one. And 56,400 people left Ontario permanently in a year. When people are leaving and you're arriving, you have less competition than you think you do.

The honest version: Nothing dramatic happened in the last 24 hours. It's a Monday before a data release. That's fine — most of investing and most of buying a home is boring days you don't panic on.
