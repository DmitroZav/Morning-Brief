CORE INFLATION AT A 5-YEAR LOW
☀️ Big Story of the Day
Inflation came in cooler than expected — and the number underneath it was the real news

Yesterday morning Statistics Canada reported that inflation slowed to 2.8% in June, down from 3.2% in May and a tick below what economists expected. Gasoline did most of the work: gas was still up 20.5% from a year ago, but that's way down from May's 33.2% spike, because pump prices dropped 10.2% in a single month.

The part almost nobody covered matters more. The Bank of Canada doesn't actually steer by the headline number — it watches "core" inflation, which strips out the wild swingers like gas so you can see the underlying trend. Both core measures fell to their lowest level in over five years: median core 1.9% (from 2.1%), trimmed-mean 1.8% (from 2.0%). Both are now below the Bank's 2% target.

Why it's video-worthy: There's a genuine contradiction sitting on the table today — inflation went down, and fixed mortgage rates went up. That gap between "the news says prices are cooling" and "my mortgage quote got worse" is exactly the confusion your audience is living in. Explaining it well is a trust-builder.

🎯 The One Thing
Core inflation just fell below the Bank of Canada's 2% target for the first time in five years — but markets still put over 90% odds on the Bank doing nothing on September 2.

Translation: the case for a rate cut is quietly getting stronger, but nobody expects one yet. If you're renewing a mortgage in the next 6 months, this is the tension to watch. TD's Leslie Preston called inflation "benign" and said the Bank can stay on the sidelines "for quite some time." Not a promise of cheaper money — a signal that the panic about rising rates is overdone.

🧠 Why It Matters (To You)

If you're renewing or buying with a fixed mortgage: yesterday's good inflation news does not lower your rate today. Fixed rates follow the 5-year bond yield, and that yield went up 7 basis points last week on oil and geopolitics — not on Canadian prices. So you're in the annoying spot where the headline says "inflation is cooling" and your broker says "rates went up." Both are true. If cooler core inflation keeps holding, bond yields usually follow it down eventually — but "eventually" is not this month's rate hold.

If you're a renter or newcomer: the practical win today is smaller and more real. Groceries are rising at 3.5% instead of 3.8%. Gas fell 10.2% in a month. And if you have kids, the bigger Canada Child Benefit hit your account yesterday — up to $8,157 a year per child under 6. That's not a headline, that's rent money.

If you're a first-time buyer in the GTA: prices are down 3.9% from a year ago, listings are down 12.9%, and sales are up 9.4%. That combination — fewer homes, more buyers — is how a soft market quietly stops being soft. The discount is real right now. It isn't guaranteed to still be there next spring.
