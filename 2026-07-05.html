☀️ Big Story of the Day
The condo "bailout" is wobbling — even the BC Premier now admits the numbers don't work

The plan everyone argued about last week — Ottawa and BC buying roughly 2,200 empty Vancouver condos and turning them into rent-to-own homes — took a strange turn this weekend. BC Premier David Eby, who is supposed to be championing it, compared the purchase to a store "liquidation sale" and admitted there are no firm plans to actually buy the Vancouver units because "the numbers just don't work" to convert them into anything affordable.

Translate that: the units sitting empty are priced so high that even buying them at a discount doesn't get you to a rent a normal family can pay. That's the whole problem with the empty-condo pile in one sentence — they were built for investors at investor prices, and there's no cheap way to un-build that.

Video-worthy because: a government program quietly admitting "the math doesn't work" is a stronger, fresher hook than the bailout debate itself. It's the honest version of the story — and honesty is your brand. Ask viewers: if even the Premier says it won't pencil out, who was this really for?

🧠 Why It Matters (To You)

First-time buyer / newcomer: The condo is usually the first rung of the ladder, and that rung just got cheaper than it's been in years — Toronto condos are near 2019 prices and, relative to income, now beat Montreal for the first time since 2010. With investors still on the sidelines, you're not bidding against a crowd. If you're pre-approved and planning to actually live in it, this is a "shop seriously" window — not a gamble on flipping.

Renter: Asking rents are down about 4.7% and have slid for 20+ straight months. That's real negotiating power — ask for a lower renewal or a free month. And with fewer new international students arriving (the study-permit cap cut new arrivals nearly in half), demand pressure on cheaper rentals is easing further.

Anyone with a job worry: Unemployment is stuck at 6.9% and the labour market is described as "stalled," with more layoffs forecast. Before you buy, that matters more than the rate — lenders stress-test your income. Lock your job situation and paperwork before you shop, because approval, not the price tag, is what stops most deals.
