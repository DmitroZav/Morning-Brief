CARNEY MEETS PREMIERS ON TARIFFS TODAY
☀️ Big Story of the Day
Trump just threatened 50% tariffs on Canadian goods — and the premiers are meeting Carney about it today

On Monday, U.S. President Donald Trump signed orders to slap 50% tariffs — a tariff is a border tax the importer pays — on a long list of Canadian goods, from cement and lumber to honey, liquor, dairy, perfumes, candles and even hockey sticks. They're set to hit August 19. It's payback for provincial bans on U.S. liquor, our protected dairy system, and quotas on some American cars.

Prime Minister Mark Carney spoke with Trump on Tuesday and said the two agreed to "intensify" trade talks, but warned "all options are on the table" if the tariffs go through. Today, July 23, Carney sits down in person with all the premiers in Charlottetown, PEI — they want to see Ottawa's actual plan, and they're calling it a "new level of urgency."

Why it's video-worthy: A tariff on cement and lumber sounds like a factory problem, but it lands in a normal person's life — building homes gets more expensive, jobs get shakier, and that flows straight into rates and rent. The trick is connecting "hockey sticks and honey" to your mortgage. That's the whole video.

🎯 The One Thing
CMHC just said Ontario is the only region in Canada where home prices will actually FALL in 2026. If you're buying here, that's leverage.

CMHC — the government's housing agency — dropped its mid-year outlook Wednesday. The headline: weak sales, fewer homes getting built, and prices declining nationally this year before a slow recovery. Their baseline sees the average Canadian home at about $675,200 in 2026, down from $679,543 in 2025. But the part that matters for us: Ontario is singled out as the one region where prices keep dropping. That's not a crash — it's a soft, buyer-friendly market. Which means if you're a first-time buyer or newcomer with a stable job and a pre-approval, you have more room to negotiate right now than you've had in years. Sellers don't hold all the cards.

🧠 Why It Matters (To You)

If you're buying: CMHC just handed you a talking point. When the government's own agency says Ontario prices are falling in 2026, you can walk into a negotiation and ask for more — a lower price, covered closing costs, a flexible closing date. In a soft market, a serious buyer with financing lined up has real power. Use it.

If you're renewing or on the fence about fixed vs. variable: Watch the tariff story closely. If those 50% tariffs actually land August 19, they can push prices back up — and that inflation scare can drag the bond yield (and your fixed rate) higher, not lower. That's the risk to a "wait and see" plan right now.

If you're new to Canada: A trade war sounds like a headline for CEOs, but it hits newcomers first — the industries that hire recent arrivals (construction, manufacturing, hospitality) are exactly the ones exposed to tariffs. A softer job market plus softer home prices is a mixed bag: harder to earn, easier to buy. Keep your job stable and your options open.
