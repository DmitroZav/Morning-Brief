PREMIERS UNITED · TARIFFS LOOM AUG 19
☀️ Big Story of the Day
Carney and the premiers wrapped their tariff meeting — "everything is on the table," and the clock is now ticking to Aug 19

Thursday in Charlottetown, PEI, Prime Minister Mark Carney sat down with all the premiers for about four hours over Trump's tariff threats. The message afterward: the country is united, Ottawa is "intensifying" talks toward one comprehensive deal, and if it falls apart, "everything is on the table" for a response. A tariff, remember, is a border tax the importer pays — and it usually gets passed on to regular people through higher prices.

Two clocks are running. A 10% U.S. tariff is already hitting some Canadian goods now (products that qualify under the CUSMA free-trade deal are exempt), and the bigger one — 50% on a long list including cement, lumber, dairy, liquor, honey and even hockey sticks — lands August 19 unless there's a deal. Carney's line on any agreement: he has to be convinced "it's worth the paper it's written on."

Why it's video-worthy: Everyone's tired of tariff headlines, so the winning angle isn't "here's what happened" — it's "here's your countdown." Aug 19 is a real date with real wallet consequences. A calm "what actually changes for you on Aug 19, and what to do before then" video cuts through the noise.

🎯 The One Thing
Canadians kept spending — retail sales rose 1% in May. Good for the economy, but it quietly kills the case for a rate cut you might be waiting on.

Statistics Canada reported Thursday that retail sales climbed 1.0% to $73.7 billion in May, with every one of the nine categories it tracks moving up, and an early estimate says June rose again. Here's why a shopping number matters to your mortgage: the Bank of Canada cuts interest rates when it's worried the economy is weak. When people keep spending like this, the Bank has less reason to cut — so if your plan was "I'll just wait for cheaper rates before I buy or renew," this data says don't hold your breath. The next rate decision is September 2, and after strong spending plus a looming tariff shock, a cut is far from guaranteed.

🧠 Why It Matters (To You)

If you're waiting to buy or renew for lower rates: Thursday's spending data is a reality check. The Bank of Canada cuts when the economy limps — and Canadians are still shopping. Pair that with a possible tariff-driven price spike, and "I'll just wait for cheaper rates" looks shakier than it did a week ago. Plan around today's rates, not a cut that may not come.

If you're a renter: Some good news that's actually yours — average asking rent is down 4.3% from a year ago, the 21st month in a row of annual declines, and landlords in Toronto and Vancouver are handing out incentives like a month or two of free rent to fill a flood of new condos. If your lease is up, it's a rare moment to negotiate. Ask.

If you're new to Canada: Watch August 19. The industries that hire newcomers first — construction, manufacturing, hospitality — are exactly the ones exposed to these tariffs. Keep your job stable, keep an emergency cushion, and don't over-stretch on a purchase right before a possible shock. Softer rents give you room to breathe while you wait and see.
