☀️ Big Story of the Day
Banks are quietly selling Toronto condos — and the people getting hurt aren't the ones you'd expect

Here's the story building all week and all over the real estate feed this weekend: a wave of Toronto and Vancouver condos is being sold by banks and lenders, not by happy owners. The numbers are ugly — Ontario "power of sale" listings (that's when a lender takes over and sells a home because the owner can't pay) are up about 59% this year, and fresh appraisals are coming in $50,000 to $150,000 below what buyers agreed to pay a few years ago. In Vancouver, some condo buyers are watching $200,000 evaporate on units they signed for at the peak.

In plain language: a lot of people bought pre-construction condos back in 2021–2022 as an investment, betting prices would keep climbing. Prices went the other way. Now those buildings are finishing, the units are worth far less than the contract price, and the "assignment" market — where you sell your spot in line before closing — has basically frozen. So the units end up sold by the bank, cheap. It's painful for the speculators. But here's the part the doom headlines skip: for a regular person who just wants a home to live in, this is quietly the cheapest door into the condo market in years.

Video-worthy because: every creator is running the scary version ("buyers losing everything," "the meltdown"). Almost nobody flips it into the honest, useful version for a normal first-time buyer: someone else's bad bet can become your reasonable first home — if you know what you're looking at.
