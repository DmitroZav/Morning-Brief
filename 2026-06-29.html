☀️ Big Story of the Day
Major Shift
Canada Is Shrinking — and It's Changing the Housing Market

For the first time since Confederation (that's when Canada became a country, in 1867), Canada's population is declining. Statistics Canada confirmed this week that the population dropped for a third consecutive quarter in Q2 2026 — we now have about 41.4 million people, down 55,000 from January. The main reason: the federal government dramatically cut immigration targets. Canada went from welcoming over 500,000 new permanent residents per year to a target of 380,000 in 2026 — and non-permanent residents (students, temporary workers) dropped by nearly 118,000 in a single quarter.

Why does this matter for housing? For years, the go-to explanation for why Canadian homes cost so much was "too many people, not enough houses." Now the equation is flipping. Fewer people means less demand for rentals and homes — especially in cities that relied heavily on international students and newcomers to fill condos. Toronto, Vancouver, and their suburbs are already feeling this.

Video-worthy because: Your audience is living this. Ukrainian newcomers who came here expecting the dream are now watching Canada change direction on immigration. This is personal, relevant, and gets comments.

🧠 Why It Matters (To You)
The "So What" for Regular People

If you're renting right now, the population decline is actually something to watch — less demand for rentals could give renters more leverage than they've had in years, especially as condo investors who bought pre-construction are starting to offload units. We already see mortgage arrears (that means people 90+ days behind on payments) at a 12-year high across Canada.

If you're thinking about buying, fixed rates are quietly drifting down even without a BoC cut — because bond markets are moving. Ron Butler posted a video today called "RATES ARE FALLING" that already has 19,000 views. That's a sign this story is resonating. Now is a good moment to at least get pre-approved and see what rate you can lock in.

If you own a Toronto condo: TD Bank just published a report today saying the condo correction in Toronto could last until 2028 — calling it the "longest correction on record." That's not a reason to panic sell, but it is a reason to have a clear plan and not assume prices bounce back quickly.
