☀️ Big Story of the Day
Controversy
Carney's Condo Bailout — $3.2 Billion of Your Taxes, Going to Developers

Prime Minister Mark Carney and BC Premier David Eby announced a $3.2 billion plan to buy up over 2,200 unsold condos in Metro Vancouver and convert them into "affordable housing." The pitch: developers built too many condos, nobody's buying them, so the government steps in to buy them at market price and re-label them affordable. Critics — including opposition leader Pierre Poilievre and mortgage broker Ron Butler — are calling it a straight-up developer bailout: public money rescuing private companies who made risky bets.

Context that matters: there are a record 4,295 newly completed but unsold condos sitting empty in the GTA right now too. This BC deal is the test case. If it becomes a national playbook, GTA developers could be next in line for a government rescue.

Video-worthy because: Ron Butler already has nearly 20,000 views on this. It's hot, it's controversial, and your audience — people who are struggling to afford housing — will have strong feelings about the government helping developers instead of buyers.

🧠 Why It Matters (To You)
The Plain-Language Takeaway

If you're a first-time buyer: The government is spending $3.2 billion rescuing condo developers in BC — but mortgage rates are still at 4%+ and the BoC isn't cutting. If you're waiting for rates to come down before buying, today's inflation number (3.2%) says that wait just got longer.

If you're a renter: With housing starts falling and population still declining, the rental market is getting a bit easier — condo rents are at 33-month lows in some cities. If you've been meaning to negotiate your rent down, the leverage is there right now.

If you're watching the condo market: 4,295 unsold completed condos sitting empty in the GTA right now. If Ottawa extends the BC bailout model to Ontario, that's your tax money going to developers who overbuilt. Worth keeping an eye on — and a very strong video topic.

If you're a newcomer or thinking about Canada: Canada's population actually shrank this quarter — for the third quarter in a row. Fewer people means softer demand for housing. That's contributing to lower rents and a flat GTA market. The story of Canada is changing in real time.
