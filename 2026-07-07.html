☀️ Big Story of the Day
The US refused to renew our big trade deal — so now it gets reviewed every single year

Here's what happened while everyone was watching the pipeline: on July 1 the three countries in our North American free-trade deal — Canada, the US, and Mexico — sat down for a mandatory review of CUSMA (the deal that replaced NAFTA). The United States declined to lock it in for another 16 years. The deal doesn't die — it still runs to 2036 — but instead of being reviewed once every six years, it now gets picked apart every single year. Yesterday (July 6), Canada's trade minister Dominic LeBlanc said Canada is "seeking clarity" and wants those annual talks to finally kill the US tariffs still hitting our steel, aluminum, autos, and lumber.

In plain language: about 90% of what Canada sells to the US still crosses the border tariff-free because of this deal — that's the good news, and it's why your job and grocery prices didn't just blow up. The bad news: the punishing tariffs on a few big industries (steel, aluminum, cars, lumber) are still there, and now the whole agreement is up for a fight every year instead of being settled for a generation. That means more uncertainty for anyone whose paycheque touches those industries — and uncertainty is exactly what makes businesses stop hiring.

Video-worthy because: everyone reported "trade deal survives" or "Trump blocks extension" as a political headline. Nobody translated it into: does my job feel safer or shakier this morning? That's your lane.
